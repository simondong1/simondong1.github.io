Understanding MLA Decoding Materialize vs Absorb

There are two ways to run Multi-head Latent Attention at inference: rebuild the cache into full keys and values, or fold the projections into the query. This post shows both, and the crossover that decides which one to use.

Simon Dong•July 2026•~13 min read

MLA stores one small latent per token. But how do you actually compute attention from that latent? There are two algorithms, and modern engines ship both. This post walks through each step by step, then uses interactive charts to show exactly when one beats the other.

1.Notation, spelled out

No single-letter shorthand in this post. Every quantity keeps its full name.

NameMeaningDeepSeek-V3
new_tokensquery tokens processed in this call1 (decode) … thousands (prefill)
cached_tokenstokens already in the KV cache (context length)up to ~128k
headsnumber of attention heads128
nope_dimcontent part of each head (no position)128
rope_dimposition (rotary) part of each head64
value_dimvalue width per head128
latent_dimcompressed KV width MLA stores per token512

The cache holds, per token: one latent vector of width latent_dim, plus one shared rope key of width rope_dim. Keys and values for every head are produced from that latent by learned projections.

2.Prefill and decode are the same computation

People treat prefill and decode as different, but for attention they are the same operation with one number changed:

decode   =  new_tokens is 1        (one fresh token attends to the whole cache)
prefill  =  new_tokens is large    (the whole prompt attends to itself)

So instead of two stories, we analyze one cost as new_tokens grows from 1 upward. The crossover between the two algorithms will fall somewhere on that axis.

3.The two algorithms

Both start from the same cached latent. They differ in where the key/value projections are applied.

The single most important difference is highlighted in red:

  • Materialize rebuilds keys and values for every cached token, so its projection work grows with cached_tokens.
  • Absorb only transforms the new queries, so its projection work grows with new_tokens.

At decode, cached_tokens is huge and new_tokens is 1. So Materialize does a huge rebuild while Absorb does almost nothing. That is the whole intuition — the charts below make it exact.

4.Cost of each step

The one rule: a matmul of shape (rows × inner) times (inner × cols) costs rows × cols × inner multiply-adds. Applying it to every step, per layer:

Approach B — Absorb (fold into the query)

StepCost (multiply-adds, per layer)Grows with
fold key into queryheads × new_tokens × nope_dim × latent_dimnew_tokens

scores (wider)heads × new_tokens × cached_tokens × (latent_dim + rope_dim)new × cached

Absorb total
fold heads × new_tokens × latent_dim × (nope_dim + value_dim)
attention heads × new_tokens × cached_tokens × (2 × latent_dim + rope_dim)

Put the two projection terms next to each other. They are the same work per token — latent_dim × (nope_dim + value_dim) — but Materialize multiplies it by cached_tokens (rebuild the whole cache) while Absorb multiplies it by new_tokens (only the fresh queries). That single swap is the entire trade-off. Absorb repays it with a wider attention term (latent_dim instead of nope_dim).

5.The trade-off, interactively

The chart plots both totals as new_tokens grows from 1 (decode) to thousands (prefill). Pick a model and drag the context length. Where the lines cross is where the winner changes.

Notice the shape: the two lines are nearly parallel on the log scale, and they cross once. Left of the cross (few new tokens = decode) Absorb is far below. Right of the cross (many new tokens = prefill) Materialize dips under. Increasing the context slides the crossover only slightly — it is close to a fixed number of tokens, not a fixed fraction of context.

6.The crossover point

Set the two totals equal and solve for new_tokens. Fully expanded:

crossover new_tokens =
        cached_tokens × latent_dim × (nope_dim + value_dim)
   ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   latent_dim × (nope_dim + value_dim)
   + cached_tokens × ( 2 × latent_dim − nope_dim − value_dim )

For long context the first term in the denominator becomes negligible, and it simplifies to a value that does not depend on context length at all:

crossover new_tokens  ≈   latent_dim × (nope_dim + value_dim)
                          ───────────────────────────────────
                          2 × latent_dim − nope_dim − value_dim

For DeepSeek-V3 that is about 170 tokens; for the smaller config about 256 tokens. The live value for your current settings is shown in the box above. The takeaway: the boundary between "rebuild the cache" and "fold into the query" sits at a couple hundred query tokens, essentially independent of how long the context is.

7.The cache-size win (why we bother)

All of this exists to keep the KV cache tiny. Storing one shared latent instead of full per-head keys and values is the reason MLA is worth the extra attention width. Per token, per layer:

For DeepSeek-V3 that is 576 stored values per token per layer (latent 512 + rope 64) versus 40,960 for a full multi-head cache — about 71× smaller. Absorb is what lets you keep only that small latent at decode instead of rebuilding and storing the full keys and values.

8.Which algorithm runs when

Situationnew_tokensvs crossoverChosen
Decode (one token at a time)1far belowAbsorb
Speculative / multi-token decode~2–8belowAbsorb
Chunked prefillchunk sizetuned near iteither, per chunk
Full prefillthousandsfar aboveMaterialize

This is exactly why a serving engine ships both paths. Decode lives far below the crossover, so it always folds the projections into the query (Absorb) and keeps the cache tiny. Prefill lives far above it, so it rebuilds keys and values once and runs the cheaper narrow attention (Materialize). Neither wins everywhere, and the crossover formula above is the dividing line.

MLA decoding in one line: rebuild-the-cache (Materialize) wins when you have many new tokens; fold-into-the-query (Absorb) wins when you have few — and decode always has few.
